Mobile lite · design philosophy
Glance-and-go briefing view, not an investigation tool.
The mobile experience answers "is anything on fire?" in 3 seconds and lets the user tap into the deep-dive for any issue. It deliberately omits everything that requires a canvas: page tabs, layer toggle, scrubber, heatmap, prompt. Those are desktop tools.
What ships on mobile
✓ Today's priority strip (full width)
✓ Health score card → tap opens full-screen Brief
✓ Top 5 friction zones (vs 3 on desktop right rail)
✓ What changed this week (3 items, condensed)
✓ Each row → mobile-formatted deep-dive page with Push to engineering
✗ No prompt bar · No canvas · No layer toggle · No scrubber · No page tabs
Navigation
Hamburger menu (top-right) opens a slide-down sheet with: Components Health · Weekly Brief · Switch to desktop view (with copy-link button). All drawer-style content from desktop becomes a full-screen sheet on mobile.
Why this works for the case study
A standout case study can claim "designed for both buyer and daily user." The director (buyer) opens Pulse on their phone Monday morning, sees the priority + health + what-changed in one scroll, taps to read more. They never touch the desktop tool. Designed for the JTBD, not for parity.

Mobile lite philosophy. The deliberate omissions ARE the design. Mobile users (typically the Director on Monday morning, or a designer checking on the train) need "is anything on fire?" in 3 seconds — they don't need to scrub time or switch layers. Stripping those tools makes mobile faster than desktop for the glance JTBD. The case study can claim "two distinct experiences, both designed for their JTBD" instead of "one experience, two viewports."

Tap behavior. Every friction card on mobile → opens the mobile-formatted deep-dive page (same data as desktop, but stacked vertically with the four metric tiles becoming a 2×2 grid, code diff scrolling horizontally, "Generate fix" still works). "Push to engineering" + "Open in Linear" CTAs at the bottom remain functional. From the deep-dive, "← Back" returns to home.
