# Hero Positioning Research (2026-06-10)

**Subject:** the homepage headline + subtitle. The user vetoed 'ships infrastructure other designers use' (tool-specialist read) and 'building like a founding team' (simile). Target identity: founding designer / designer #1 with full zero-to-one breadth.

**Method:** two parallel sweeps: (1) verbatim hero lines from 14 elite designer/design-engineer portfolios, (2) founding-designer hiring language from First Round, Designer Fund, Andy Budd, YC job posts (Arnata, FurtherAI, Centralize), Wellfound, Unusual VC.


## Chosen line (shipped)

**Headline:** Product designer who ships the whole loop: research, design, production code.

**Subtitle:** San Jose. An enterprise token pipeline at LexisNexis Risk Solutions, two AI products built solo in code, a brand run to 200+ sales. Building in Claude Code (/process). Looking for designer #1 roles that grade on what ships.

**Why:** verbs of making + the four founder screening phrases (whole loop/generalist, ships, code, designer #1); every clause carries a number, proper noun, or artifact; closes with the direct ask (Delba precedent); 'grades on what ships' echoes Centralize's 'We grade on what's in production, not what's in Figma.'

## Patterns

- Name as headline, one sentence as positioning: the dominant elite pattern is the bare name (or name flowing grammatically into a sentence, like Rauno's) followed by ONE declarative sentence of 9-20 words stating role + current employer/product + optionally city. The headline itself is almost never a tagline.

- Proper nouns are the credibility engine: every strong line contains at least one named anchor — employer (Vercel, Linear, Cursor, Shopify, Perplexity), place (Estonia, Ireland, New York), or product. The named thing replaces the adjective: 'working with Vercel' says what 'world-class' tries to say.

- Verbs of making, present tense: build, craft, design, ship, work on, experiment. Never verbs of being or feeling ('I am passionate', 'I love'). Paco's all-gerund fragment rhythm (Crafting. Building. Experimenting.) is the purest form — the verbs imply the person without a single 'I am'.

- Micro-claims about detail beat macro-claims about impact: the most-quoted lines in this cohort are taste claims at the smallest scale — 'details that most people never notice but always feel' (Salaja), 'how it looks, feels, behaves' (Emil), 'people who sweat the details' (MDS). Hiring managers can't verify 'impact'; they can feel a detail claim instantly.

- Numbers and career arc over adjectives: 'coding for 15 years and teaching for the second half' (leerob) does narrative in 11 words. One number outperforms three adjectives.

- Short sentence stacks, not one long sentence: supporting copy reads as 2-4 sentences of <=12 words each, comma-light, clause-light. Total supporting copy stays under ~35 words on the strongest sites.

- Audience qualifier as differentiator: naming WHO it's for ('for designers and developers' — Emil; 'for people who sweat the details' — MDS) self-selects the reader and implies positioning without claiming superiority.

- The stronger the work, the shorter the hero: Benji ships NO tagline; Emmi Wu and Josh Comeau ship NO hero prose at all and open on the artifact grid. Hero copy length is inversely proportional to demo strength — which matches this portfolio's existing Case Study Opener rule (artifact first, prose after).

- Honesty/warmth moves are allowed but only AFTER the concrete intro: 'Hi! I'm Maxime' and 'unfinished or imperfect projects' work because role+city landed in the same breath. Personality is the seasoning, never the substance.

- The direct ask is a legitimate hero when job-seeking: Delba leads with 'I'm looking for my next role in developer education... If you're hiring: Let's talk' — named target role + CTA. Relevant precedent for a founding-designer-targeting portfolio.


## Anti-patterns

- Generic title soup: pipe- or slash-separated identity lists ('Designer | Developer | Dreamer'). Zero occurrences across all 14 elite sites — the role is always one noun phrase, max two ('designer and developer at Vercel').

- Passion-claims and love-claims: 'passionate about', 'with a love for', 'I enjoy pushing boundaries'. The two weakest sentences in this entire survey are Salaja's 'pushing boundaries and exploring new ways to express myself without limitations' and Emmi's 'with a love for' — both survive only because a concrete sentence sits next to them.

- Abstraction without an anchor: 'crafting digital experiences', 'design is a dialogue between users, designers, and developers' (Linus Rogge), 'explore the power of the web' (Jhey's tail). Any clause where you cannot point at the named thing being discussed is filler. Test: does the clause contain a proper noun, a number, or a concrete artifact type? If none, cut it.

- Asserting a quality instead of demonstrating it: 'Subtle, but with great impact' (Rogge) tells the reader what to conclude. Elite copy never grades its own work — it names the work and lets the artifact grade it.

- Welcome filler as headline: 'Welcome to my portfolio / my corner of the Internet' fails as a first line. Maxime's version survives only in the supporting slot, after role+city already landed.

- Bureaucratic connective tissue: 'My interests span a broad spectrum of subjects, encompassing...' (Shu Ding) — 8 words of scaffolding around a list that could stand alone. List the nouns; delete 'span/encompass/ranging from/various'.

- Near-cliché frames even on elite sites: 'at the intersection of X and Y' (MDS) and 'magical details' (Paco) are the survey's two borderline phrases — both carried by concrete neighbors. Do not use them without an immediately adjacent proper-noun anchor; better, avoid them.

- Simile/metaphor-based self-description: 'design is my superpower', 'I turn coffee into code', 'bridging the gap between...' — entirely absent from this cohort. Metaphor invites skepticism; the named employer/artifact invites verification.

- Long multi-clause hero sentences (30+ words): the only 30+ word constructions found were the weakest lines (Rogge's manifesto, Shu's spectrum sentence). Elite ceiling is ~20 words per sentence, ~35 words total supporting copy.

- Clever tagline that requires decoding before the role lands: none of the 14 sites make the reader work out what the person does. Role + employer is parseable in under 2 seconds on every strong site — wordplay, if any, comes after comprehension, never instead of it.


## What founders screen for

- Generalist range across the whole design surface (product UX, UI, brand, marketing, research). Designer Fund's hiring guide recommends generalists who 'own projects end-to-end across disciplines'; their founding-designer interviews warn that 'someone with a narrow speciality would be hard pressed to carry the weight of moving the company forward.' Wellfound posts ask for 'a generalist, T-shaped product designer with acuity across all pillars from UX research to Visual Design.' (designerfund.com, wellfound.com)

- Zero-to-one comfort and ambiguity tolerance. First Round's Ultimate Guide frames the role around the gap between 'what is' and 'what could be'; Andy Budd defines the hire as 'a fast, product-minded generalist who can help you go from zero to one... someone who can take vague ideas, messy whiteboards, and half-built code' and make them usable. Arnata's YC post asks for '4+ years designing real, shipped product experiences, ideally from 0 to 1.' (review.firstround.com, andybudd.com, ycombinator.com)

- Speed and shipping bias over polish. First Round: 'Speed is not a dirty word' and 'losing sight of good for perfect is not a privilege available to a design team of one.' Andy Budd: 'They care about quality—but they know shipped is better than perfect.' Designer Fund 2025: designers who 'iterate in hours instead of weeks.' (review.firstround.com, andybudd.com, designerfund.substack.com)

- Ownership and a founder's mindset. FurtherAI's YC founding-designer post screens for 'Above all: drive, grit, and ownership' and explicitly wants someone who may 'start your own company in the future.' First Round notes founding designers 'have every title and simultaneously no title at all.' Founders read prior founding (a brand, a side business, shipped indie products) as the strongest proxy. (workatastartup.com/ycombinator.com, review.firstround.com)

- Product thinking, not deliverables thinking. Arnata: 'Strong product thinking — you design why, not just how.' Studio Contrast: the first designer must 'think like a product manager and contribute to strategy, not just design.' Unusual VC tells founders to hire 'deep thinkers' who were 'the fiercest advocates for our customers' state of mind' and who have shipped 3-4 real products. (ycombinator.com, contrast.studio, unusual.vc)

- Code ability, increasingly explicit in 2025-2026 posts. Centralize's founding-designer post requires 'Fluency building in code (React, modern CSS) and shipping your own designs to production... whether by hand or with tools like Claude Code,' and states 'The dream hire ships their own work to production' and 'We grade on what's in production, not what's in Figma.' Sunit Singh's 2026 essay: the best founding designers are 'comfortable getting close to code... not just making mockups; they are shaping real product behaviour.' (remotive.com/Centralize via Ashby, medium.com/@surdattack)

- AI-tool fluency as a first-class signal. Designer Fund's 2025 founder guide wants 'full-stack designers with strong visual craft, business understanding, and the ability to design with and for AI' who are already 'experimenting with AI tools to ship faster.' Job posts now name tools (Claude Code, Cursor) directly. (designerfund.substack.com, remotive.com)

- Taste and a high craft bar. Arnata: 'Taste. Yes, capital-T Taste. The kind you can't fake with Dribbble shots' plus 'A portfolio that makes us pause, not scroll.' Centralize: 'Sharp visual taste. You can look at a screen and see what's wrong.' (ycombinator.com, remotive.com)

- Systems thinking that scales beyond the first hire. FurtherAI: 'Build a cohesive design system that will scale across products.' Centralize: 'Build and evolve our design system.' First Round's guide flags systems thinking and building scalable process before the team exists. (ycombinator.com, remotive.com, review.firstround.com)

- Hands-on research and customer contact. Centralize expects the founding designer on sales calls and user testing; Unusual VC weights user advocacy heavily; Designer Fund tells founders to probe how candidates connect research to roadmap decisions. (remotive.com, unusual.vc, designerfund.com)

- Brutal prioritization and autonomy. Designer Fund interviewee: 'You need to be really good at prioritizing, almost brutally so. No one's going to tell you exactly what to do' and 'There's a million question marks around you and you have to be able to be ok deciding which question mark to work on each day.' (designerfund.com)

- Lead-level but not big-co process-heavy. Andy Budd's archetype: 'someone in the middle: a lead-level designer' who is 'comfortable jumping between strategy and bug fixes,' often 'the third or fourth design hire at a fast-growth startup,' explicitly NOT a FAANG specialist. Designer Fund recommends 4+ years minimum; Unusual VC says 3-4 shipped products. (andybudd.com, designerfund.com, unusual.vc)

## Positioning advice (apply beyond the hero: About, case intros)

- HEADLINE (one line, first viewport): "Product designer who ships the whole loop: research, design, and production code." This hits the four screening phrases founders actually use (generalist loop, product-minded, ships, code) in 11 words. A close variant keyed to zero-to-one language: "Product designer who takes products from zero to one, in Figma and in code." Either reads as founding-designer breadth, not tool specialization. The current line ('ships systems, products, and the prototypes in between') is good but leads with 'systems', which nudges the tools-specialist read; put 'products' or 'the whole loop' first.

- SUPPORTING PARAGRAPH (directly under the headline, ~4 sentences, every clause carries a number or an artifact): "I work the full zero-to-one loop. At LexisNexis I ran the research (8 analyst interviews that reshaped the roadmap), then designed and built the Figma-to-Storybook token pipeline that cut palette updates from a full day to 30 seconds. On my own time I designed and coded two AI-native products end to end (Pulse and Ghost) in vanilla JS, building in Claude Code, and founded an apparel brand with 200+ sales. I'm looking to be designer #1 somewhere that grades on what ships." The closing line deliberately echoes Centralize's 'We grade on what's in production, not what's in Figma.'

- Mirror the job-post vocabulary verbatim. Founders screen with specific strings: 'end to end', 'zero to one', 'ship to production', 'founder's mindset', 'own the entire product design process', 'taste'. Using these exact phrases in the headline, About page, and case-study intros makes the portfolio pattern-match in the 30-second scan. Naming Claude Code explicitly is now a real keyword: at least one live founding-designer post (Centralize) names it in the requirements.

- De-risk the tools-specialist read by reframing the subject of each project. Pulse and Ghost should be introduced as 'AI-native products I designed and built end to end in code' (the proof is the zero-to-one execution; the design-tools domain is incidental), not as 'design tooling concepts.' LexisNexis should lead with the business outcome and the cross-functional bridge (design + engineering + research), with the pipeline as the artifact. GoteFigure is the highest-leverage founding signal on the page: 'founded a brand, 200+ sales' is literal evidence of the founder's mindset FurtherAI-style posts screen for; never bury it as 'a fun side project.'

- Put metrics in the first viewport, because founders grade on outcomes, not process. 'Full day to 30 seconds', '8 interviews to roadmap', '2 products built in code', '200+ sales' are exactly the 'clear line of sight between their work and business results' Designer Fund tells founders to hire for. A founding-designer reader should hit a number within the first scroll.

- Show range as a deliberate structure, not a coincidence: one shipped-at-work systems story (LexisNexis), two built-in-code product stories (Pulse, Ghost), one founded-business story (GoteFigure) is precisely the 'every title and no title at all' generalist shape First Round describes. A single connective sentence on the homepage or About page ('research, systems, products, and a business: the four jobs of a designer #1') turns the portfolio from 'four projects' into an argument for the role.
